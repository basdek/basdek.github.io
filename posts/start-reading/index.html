Start a blog, begin more structured learning

March 10, 2019 3 minutes read

Hello, I am Bas

I have been working in software professionally (i.e. getting paid to write software of some sorts) for over six years. I have been interested in software for as long as I can remember. My father sparked this interest back when I was 11 years old, when he showed me the possibilities of Microsoft Excel… One could say that he gave me a natural predisposition towards reactive programming :)

When the Excel macros stopped having secrets for me I wanted to move on to a real programming language and PHP served that purpose (for a while…) Ever since, I self thaught everything I needed to know to get a specific thing, the thing at hand done. Reading blogposts and the occcasional compscie paper, discussing with friends I made in the tech scene, discussing with colleagues I encountered at various places of work are my primary methods for knowledge extension.

A discussion with one of my dearest friends in software and life, let to the insight that the study method of “looking it up as soon as you need it” works to some extend, but can also be limitting. For him it works longer, as his natural inquisitiveness takes him so deep into the rabbit hole he easily outlearns me — and many others, for that matter. We discussed that a difference between us exists in the fact that to learn the same thing, to get a strong intuition for the same concept (programming and the practical application of knowledge in general is very much about intuition I believe) I need to take a more studious, disciplined, structured approach than he does. There is no shame in that. I am actually looking forward to studying some books very thoroughly, doing their excercises or inventing my own.

The process of learning anything that has to do with software engineering interests me for other purposes than broadening my own knowledge, too: As an (in)formal mentor to teammates of various experience and skill levels transfering my knowledge is often a part of my work. Especially with people that are less experienced and don’t quite grasp certain fundamentals yet it is difficult to explain the concepts in a way that will grow their intuition. For a lot of fundamentals I forgot how I learned them and have difficulty explaining. So I hope that by going on a journey of learning in a structured way myself I might become a better teacher. Improving my teaching skills is a good reason to invest time in this blog. Both documenting how I learn and what I learned will hopefully help me understand learning better.

Let’s put my money where my mouth is. I bought a couple of books to study and will hopefully soon write on my progress with them:

  • “Functional and reactive domain modeling” Debasish Ghosh
  • “Functional programming in Scala” Paul Chiusano and Rúnar Bjarnason

I might also reread the previously acquired “Grokking algorithms” by the fantastic Aditya Y. Bhargava, until further notice the author that wins the price for best (graphical) explanation of the essential typeclasses in functional programming.

That Aditya Y. Bhargava blogpost is a perfect example of my motivation for blogging about learning, learning processes and teaching. Why did his blog work for me and so many other explanations not? I don’t really know this anymore, because I did not document the experience and cannot recollect it to the degree necessary to make an accurate conclusion.

Ample reasons to write, to read, to study and to document. Let’s start!
